# Lore

## Purpose

Contains story elements. Lore should support gameplay. Lore should never dictate gameplay.

---

# Setting

**Era:** Late 19th to early 20th century — the Victorian and Edwardian periods (approximately 1880–1914).

This was the golden age of geographical and archaeological discovery. Explorer societies united scientists, adventurers, and naturalists in the pursuit of knowledge — and treasure. Everything in the game must be consistent with this era: no items, knowledge, or technology newer than this period exists in the world.

**Inspirations:**
- The Royal Geographical Society (founded 1830) — sponsored expeditions of Livingstone and Scott
- The National Geographic Society (founded 1888)
- The Explorers Club, New York (founded 1904)
- The Adventurers' Club, Los Angeles (founded 1912)

---

# World Premise

Many stories thought to be myths are real.

The player is a Victorian-era explorer and member of an Explorers Club — an organisation that, on its surface, unites globe-trotting adventurers, scientists, and naturalists. In practice, the club is a front for something older: a secret order with a history stretching back centuries, initiated into knowledge that contradicts the official historical record.

The player may not know this at first. By the end of the game, they will.

---

# Narrative Themes

- Greed and its consequences
- Curiosity as courage and as danger
- Fear of the unknown
- Discovery that recontextualises what you thought you knew
- Survival against something that cannot be defeated
- Initiation — the gradual recognition that you are part of something larger

---

# Why Creatures Guard Treasure

The creatures are not random monsters. They are eternal guardians — bound to their location and its contents by ancient covenant, ritual, or curse. The treasure they guard was never meant to leave: it is sacred tribute, the property of a civilisation, or the physical anchor of the creature's existence.

From the creature's perspective, the Victorian explorer is a thief and a violator of ancient law.

---

# Who Built These Locations

Ancient civilisations — Greek, Norse, Egyptian, Japanese, and others — constructed these places with intent. They were not merely temples or burial sites. They were repositories of knowledge, ritual spaces, and containment structures for things that should not be disturbed. The fact that they still function millennia later is not an accident.

---

# Are the Myths Connected?

Yes. This is the meta-secret.

Individual levels appear to be isolated mythology — a Greek labyrinth, a Norse ruin, an Egyptian tomb. But lore collected across levels is intertwined. Pieces of knowledge found in separate locations, assembled together, point toward a larger truth: a pre-civilisation common origin, a suppressed site, or the real history of the secret order itself.

This is the meta-quest. Most players will never complete it. Those who do earn the title of the initiated.

---

# The Meta-Quest

**The game is the initiation.**

The player completes expeditions, collects artifacts, solves sidequests, and assembles lore — believing they are doing this for the club, for science, for score. What they are actually doing is completing an initiation ritual that spans ancient mythologies and centuries of secret history.

**Three possible meta-quest threads (Future Vision — design now, build later):**

**1. The common origin**
Greek, Norse, Egyptian, and Japanese mythologies share structural similarities that cannot be coincidence. The lore fragments across levels reveal why: a single pre-historical civilisation seeded all of them. The creatures are not separate myths — they are the same myth, remembered differently.

**2. The suppressed location**
Each mythology's sidequest lore provides one fragment of a location — a direction, a landmark, a coordinate in an unknown system. Assembled, they point to a place that should not exist and that the order has known about for centuries. Era-specific searches (the source of the Nile, the Holy Grail, Blackbeard's treasure) may be deliberate misdirections protecting this true location.

**3. The order's true history**
Each level's hidden lore names a figure, an era, a purpose. Assembled, they reveal who founded the order, what they found, and what they are still searching for — and whether the player was always part of this or was drawn in unknowingly.

**The "never reached" principle:**
These meta-quests are designed to be approached but perhaps never fully resolved — consistent with the era's great unsolved expeditions. The initiated know more than the uninitiated. Whether the final truth is reached is left open.

**Scope:** Future Vision. Design it now so every piece of level lore is written knowing it may contribute to this.
